The roadmap

Valuation is just answering one question with discipline: what is this business worth, and is the price asking too much for it? Every method below is a different route to that answer. They split into two families.

Family 01

Relative — multiples

Price the company against its peers. PE, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA. Fast, market-grounded, and how most desks actually screen. The catch: if the whole sector is mispriced, the multiple inherits that error.

Family 02

Intrinsic — cash flow

Build value from the company's own future cash: DCF and DDM. Independent of what the market thinks today. The catch: the answer is only as good as your assumptions, and small inputs swing it hard.

Climb the rungs in order, or jump to any one. Each rung tells you what it measures, who it suits, and where it lies to you.

PE ratio

beginner

Price-to-earnings is the most widely quoted valuation metric in the world. Understanding it properly, flaws included, is foundational.

▦ What it is

The PE ratio tells you how much investors are willing to pay for each naira (or dollar) of earnings.

PE = Share price ÷ Earnings per share (EPS)

A PE of 15 means investors are paying ₦15 for every ₦1 of annual profit the company makes. Think of it as years of earnings — at PE 15, you are paying for 15 years of current earnings.

◴ Trailing vs forward PE

Trailing PE uses the last 12 months of actual reported EPS. Based on known facts: more reliable, but backward-looking.

Forward PE uses the next 12 months of estimated EPS. More relevant for valuation, but it depends on analyst forecasts being accurate.

Always state which one you are using. They can look very different, especially for fast-growing or recovering businesses.

▤ How to interpret it

Low PE (below sector average) could mean undervalued — but could also mean the market is pricing in declining earnings. Always ask why.

High PE means the market expects strong earnings growth. The question is whether that growth will materialise.

Negative PE means the company is loss-making. PE is meaningless here — use other metrics.

Real example — MTNN

MTNN: price ₦800, FY2025 EPS ₦53.07 → trailing PE = 15.1x. Nigerian telecom peers average 18–22x, so on this metric MTNN looks modestly undervalued.

But remember: FY2025 was the first profit after a ₦399bn loss year. The PE is low partly because EPS is recovering from a very low base. The forward PE on FY2026 estimated EPS matters far more here.

⚠ PE traps — common mistakes

trap 1

Comparing PE across different sectors. A bank at PE 8x is not cheaper than a tech company at PE 30x. Growth rates, capital requirements and risks are fundamentally different. Always compare within sector.

trap 2

One-off items distorting EPS. UACN's PE of 50x looked alarming — but EPS was crushed by ₦21bn of one-off CHI acquisition costs. Normalised PE was far lower. Always read the notes.

trap 3

Cyclical stocks at peak earnings. Mining and oil companies look cheap at low PE when earnings peak — but that is exactly when you should be cautious, not greedy.

trap 4

Ignoring debt. Two companies can have identical PE ratios but one carries massive debt. PE ignores the balance sheet entirely. Always pair it with EV/EBITDA.

Why forward PE wins for a recovering company

Trailing PE looks backward. For a business climbing out of a loss, last year's depressed EPS makes the trailing multiple noisy — sometimes flatteringly low, sometimes meaninglessly high.

Take the MTNN case. At ₦800 the trailing PE is 15.1x on FY2025 EPS of ₦53.07. But if FY2026 EPS recovers toward, say, ₦100 as the naira stabilises (illustrative, not a forecast), the forward PE drops to roughly:

800 ÷ 100 = 8.0x forward

Same price, very different story — 8x forward versus 15x trailing. The market is not valuing what the company earned; it is valuing what it is about to earn. For turnarounds, banks coming off a provisioning cycle, or anyone with a low earnings base, forward is the honest number — as long as you trust the estimate behind it.

P/B & P/S

beginner

When earnings are unreliable or absent, two cousins of PE step in: price-to-book for asset-heavy businesses, and price-to-sales for companies not yet making money.

Price-to-Book (P/B)

P/B = Share price ÷ Book value per share

Book value is what's left for shareholders on the balance sheet — assets minus liabilities. P/B compares the market price to that accounting net worth.

Best for banks and asset-heavy firms, where the balance sheet is the business. Nigerian banks frequently trade below 1.0x book — GTCO, Zenith and peers have spent long stretches priced under their own net assets.

Price-to-Sales (P/S)

P/S = Market cap ÷ Annual revenue

Revenue is much harder to manipulate than earnings, and it exists even when profit doesn't.

Best for high-growth or pre-profit companies where PE is negative or meaningless. The fatal flaw: P/S completely ignores whether those sales make money. A loss-making firm and a fat-margin one can share a P/S.

⚠ Traps

P/B

Book value can be fiction. It ignores intangibles (a software firm's real worth) and assumes the assets are worth their stated value. A bank's book is only as good as its loan book — discount it if bad debts are lurking.

P/S

Sales without margins are vanity. Two firms at P/S of 2x are not comparable if one converts 30% to profit and the other loses money on every sale. Never use P/S without checking the margin alongside it.

EV multiples

intermediate

PE's biggest blind spot is debt. Enterprise value fixes it by valuing the whole business — what it would cost to buy outright, debt and all — not just the equity slice.

What enterprise value is

EV = Market cap + Total debt − Cash

If you bought the entire company, you'd pay for the equity, inherit its debt, but pocket its cash. That net figure is what the business truly costs. EV multiples then divide it by a profit measure:

EV/EBITDA — the workhorse. Strips out financing and accounting noise, so you can compare companies with very different debt loads.  EV/EBIT — stricter, counts depreciation.  EV/Sales — for the pre-profit case.

Why it matters on the NGX

Take a leveraged name like Seplat. On PE it can look ordinary, but it carries real debt. EV folds that debt into the price tag, so EV/EBITDA shows the true cost of owning the cash flows. For capital-heavy Nigerian names — cement, oil, telecoms infrastructure — EV multiples are usually fairer than PE.

⚠ Traps

trap 1

EBITDA is not cash flow. It ignores capex, working capital, interest and tax. For a cement or oil business that reinvests heavily, EBITDA flatters reality — a lot of it never reaches shareholders.

trap 2

Mind the debt direction. Net cash (more cash than debt) makes EV smaller than market cap. Heavy debt makes EV balloon. The same EBITDA can look cheap or expensive depending entirely on the balance sheet you bolted on.

DCF

intermediate

A discounted cash flow ignores the market entirely and asks the purest question: what is all the future cash this business will generate worth in today's money?

The idea in one line

A naira next year is worth less than a naira today — you could have invested today's naira. DCF projects the company's free cash flow for a few years, adds a terminal value for everything after, and discounts it all back at a rate that reflects risk.

Value = Σ ( future cash flow ÷ (1 + r)year ) + terminal value

Three inputs decide everything: the cash flow, the growth rate, and the discount rate (r) — your required return. In Nigeria, r is high: inflation, naira risk and rates push it past 20%, and that quietly crushes the value of cash arriving years from now.

⚠ Traps

trap 1

False precision. A DCF spits out ₦412.68 and feels authoritative. It isn't. Change r by two points and it's a different number. Treat the output as a range, never a point.

trap 2

Terminal value dominates. Often half or more of the answer sits in the terminal value — a guess about the far future. Watch the "terminal share" readout above; when it's 60%+, you're mostly valuing speculation.

trap 3

Garbage in, garbage out. It's easy to reverse-engineer the inputs until the DCF agrees with the price you wanted. Set your assumptions first, then look at the answer.

DDM

intermediate

For a company that pays a steady, growing dividend, you can value it off that payout alone. The dividend discount model is a DCF stripped down to the cash that actually reaches you.

The Gordon Growth Model

Value = Next year's dividend ÷ ( r − g )

Where r is your required return and g is the dividend's long-run growth rate. The model only works when g is below r — a dividend growing faster than your discount rate forever would be worth infinity, which is the model politely telling you the assumption is wrong.

Tailor-made for the NGX dividend payers. Nigerian banks and names like Dangote Cement are dividend machines with long payout histories — exactly the stable, predictable profile the DDM was built for.

⚠ Traps

trap 1

The r − g spread is everything. As g creeps toward r, the denominator shrinks and the value explodes. A model this sensitive to two estimates demands conservative, defensible inputs.

trap 2

No dividend, no model. Useless for growth companies that reinvest everything, and dangerous for firms paying a dividend they can't sustain. Check that the payout is actually covered by earnings.

Advanced

advanced

The pros rarely trust one number. They triangulate cheap-vs-expensive with what-is-priced-in, and they build a margin of safety into the answer.

Adjust the multiple for growth — PEG

A raw PE punishes fast growers and flatters slow ones. PEG divides PE by the earnings growth rate to level the field. A rule of thumb from Peter Lynch: PEG around 1.0 is fair — a PE of 30 is reasonable if earnings grow 30%.

PEG = PE ÷ earnings growth rate (%)

Reverse the valuation

Instead of computing a value, flip it: take today's price and solve for the growth or return the market is assuming. If the price implies 25% growth forever and the company has never grown above 10%, the market is dreaming — and you have your edge.

This is the single most useful advanced move: valuation becomes a question about expectations, not a price target.

Sum-of-the-parts

For conglomerates — think Transcorp or UAC — one blended multiple hides everything. Value each division on its own appropriate metric, add them, subtract net debt and a holding-company discount. The parts are often worth more than the quoted whole.

Margin of safety

Every method above is an estimate dressed as a number. The discipline that survives that uncertainty is simple: only buy when price sits comfortably below your range of fair value — 20–30% below for a Nigerian risk profile. The gap is not greed; it's the room your assumptions are allowed to be wrong in.

The skill isn't knowing the metrics — it's choosing the right one for the business in front of you, then refusing to skip a step.
